EXIM overview and trade flow.

exim
export import

Every document has a job. The proforma invoice starts the deal, the commercial invoice becomes the final bill, the packing list describes how the goods are packed, and the shipping bill is the customs document that lets goods leave India. The bill of lading or airway bill proves that the carrier received the cargo, and the certificate of origin tells which country the goods came from. These documents are the paper trail of the shipment.

DGFT, IEC, HS code, and import/export policy basics.

DGFT = the Indian foreign trade authority.

IEC = Importer-Exporter Code, the trade ID of the firm.

HS code = product classification number.

Policy decides whether a product is free, restricted, canalized, or prohibited.

Wrong code or missing registration can cause problems later.

  1. Tiny real-life story

A company wants to export a ceramic item. First it needs an IEC, then the product must be classified correctly with an HS code, and then the business must follow the right policy path for that product. Once this base is correct, the rest of the export process becomes much smoother.

  1. Common mistake to avoid

Do not think DGFT is the customs office, and do not think IEC is the same as HS code. DGFT is the authority, IEC is the business number, and HS code is the product number. Keeping these three separate in your mind will make the rest of EXIM much easier

Export documents and what each one means.

Proforma invoice = first quote.

Commercial invoice = final bill.

Packing list = packing details.

Shipping bill = customs export clearance document.

Bill of lading = sea transport document.

Airway bill = air transport document.

Certificate of origin = origin proof.

LC and bill of exchange are payment documents.

Customs, shipping, CHA, and clearance flow.

Customs checks legal compliance and matching documents.

CHA helps file and manage customs clearance.

Shipping bill starts the customs export filing.

Assessment and examination may happen before approval.

LEO means the export is allowed.

Stuffing means container loading and sealing.

Freight forwarder moves the cargo; CHA clears it.

Packaging, labeling, and dispatch basics.

Packaging protects the goods and is especially important for fragile ceramics.

Double boxing and strong cushioning are common safe methods.

Labels should show handling instructions, origin, and destination.

Customs may inspect cargo, so packaging should allow safe reopening and resealing.

Fumigation is usually about wood/pest compliance, not changing the ceramic itself.

Trade finance, payment methods, and risk.

Trade finance solves the gap between shipment and payment.

LC is the safest common bank-backed method for exporters.

Documentary collection is cheaper but gives less protection than LC.

Open account is buyer-friendly but risky for the exporter.

Advance payment is best for exporter safety.

Product 3: Duralox 92 to 99.7

Duralox is Jyoti Ceramic’s high-alumina technical ceramic family, used for wear parts, lining bricks, grinding media, and other industrial components. In simple terms, it is a strong ceramic material made to survive wear, heat, and heavy industrial use.

The “92 to 99.7” refers to alumina content levels, where higher alumina generally means higher purity and performance for tougher applications.

Main use cases Duralox is used where the customer needs wear resistance, purity, hardness, and heat resistance. Common applications include ball mill lining, grinding media, wear liners, electrical or thermal insulation parts, and high-temperature industrial components.

Example If a company runs a ball mill and the steel shell gets damaged by continuous abrasion, Duralox lining bricks can protect the shell and keep the grinding process pure and efficient.

Why customers buy it Customers buy Duralox because it is hard, durable, chemically stable, and built for severe industrial conditions. The technical data shown for Duralox 92W includes high hardness, very low water absorption, high flexural and compressive strength, and safe use at high temperatures. That makes it useful where metal would wear out too fast.

How to explain it simply You can explain Duralox like this:

It is a heavy-duty industrial ceramic.

It protects equipment from wear and contamination.

It can be made into many shapes.

It works well in mills, furnaces, and high-wear systems.

Higher alumina grades are generally stronger and more premium.

What you must know to sell it To sell Duralox properly, ask:

What exactly is the application?

Is it for lining, grinding, insulation, or a custom part?

What temperature will it face?

What material is it contacting?

Do they need standard size or drawing-based custom parts?

Do they want 92%, 95%, 96%, 99.7%, or another grade ?

Selling logic If the customer needs wear protection in a mill, Duralox lining bricks are a strong answer. If they need grinding media, Duralox beads or related media may be more suitable. If they need an even higher-purity or higher-performance ceramic part, you move toward the higher alumina grades.

Simple customer story A mineral-processing plant is losing money because the mill lining wears out quickly. Duralox bricks protect the mill shell from abrasion and help maintain product purity. That reduces downtime and maintenance cost.

Key points to remember Form: lining bricks, tiles, media, beads, tubes, rods, and custom parts.

Function: wear protection, grinding support, insulation, and high-temperature use.

Best for: ball mills, wear zones, and demanding industrial systems.

Strengths: hard, strong, low-porosity, heat-resistant, chemically stable.

Selling tip: always ask the application before quoting a grade.
